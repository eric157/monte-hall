Bayesian Posterior Means

Intuition First (Beginner Friendly)

You choose one door. At that moment, your chosen door has only a 1 in 3 chance of hiding gold. The other two doors together hold the remaining 2 in 3 chance.

Then the host opens one of those two other doors, but only a goat door. He never reveals gold. So he removes a losing option and leaves one unopened alternative. That remaining alternative keeps most of the probability mass.

Switching is strong because it effectively bets on your first choice being wrong, which happens 2/3 of the time.

Formal Model (Math + Statistics)

Define random variables: prize door P, first choice C, host reveal H, and switch door S. Sampling and constraints are:

P, C ~ Uniform({1,2,3}), with H != C and H != P, and S != C, S != H.

Win events are:

W_stay = {C = P}, W_switch = {S = P}.

Since P(C=P)=1/3, we get P(W_stay)=1/3 and P(W_switch)=2/3. Under repeated trials, empirical rates converge by the Law of Large Numbers.

How We Track Learning: Frequentist + Bayesian

Frequentist view:

stayRate = stayWins / trials, switchRate = switchWins / trials.

Bayesian view:

p_stay ~ Beta(alpha_stay, beta_stay), p_switch ~ Beta(alpha_switch, beta_switch), starting from Beta(1,1) priors.

After each trial we update success/failure counts and plot posterior means: E[p] = alpha / (alpha + beta). This gives a smooth learning curve that still converges near 0.333 (stay) and 0.667 (switch).
